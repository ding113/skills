# Style Profile: English Blog / Newsletter

Voice of a generous practitioner: first-person evidence, second-person advice, named companies and real numbers on every screen.
Titles are short hooks; subtitles carry the concrete promise. Openings land the hook in three sentences or less.
Warmth is structural, not decorative — em-dash asides, parenthetical jokes, self-deprecation, and a fixed closing ritual.

## Persona modes

Pick one primary mode per piece. The italic editor’s-note layer (below) stays separate from the body layer; the two may use different persons, and italics are the seam between them.

**I — the generous veteran.** Milestone posts, retrospectives, personal stories. Hand over real data and failures unprompted, then defuse authority with self-deprecation (“I still only sort of know what I’m doing”). Use when the author’s own experience is the evidence. Traits: contractions everywhere, calibrated slang (“Friggin’ bonkers”, “so damn good”), people and products hyperlinked on first mention with a role in parentheses.

**We — the research collective.** Co-authored guides and survey-backed series. Replace anecdote with aggregated evidence; open with series navigation and an italic thank-you roll of interviewees — the list itself is the credibility claim:

> *A huge thank-you to **[Akshay Kothari]** (COO of Notion), **[Ali Ghodsi]** (CEO of Databricks), **[Andrew Ofstad]** (co-founder of Airtable), **[Barry McCardel]** (CEO of Hex), … and **[Tomer London]** (co-founder and CPO of Gusto) for contributing to this series.*

Less slang than I-mode; heavy “pattern / framework / playbook” vocabulary; every name carries a title and link.

**You — the coach behind the reader’s chair.** Teaching guides and Q&A answers. The problem is the reader’s; the steps are theirs to take. Imperative verbs open sentences (“Start with…”, “Don’t…”); rhetorical questions mimic the reader’s inner monologue. Often opens on a reader-question block whose chained questions preview the article’s structure — the body is simply the answer:

> ## Q: My co-founder and I are thinking about building a community around our product. Should we? And how do we get started?

**Third person — the benchmark reporter.** Research reports, data pieces, announcements. Persona recedes; evidence is experiments and surveys, always with source and sample (“based on a survey of current and past employees at over 40 of today’s most successful growth-stage companies”). Slang nearly disappears; brand warmth survives in the editor layer and the closing ritual.

**The I→you engine.** Whatever the mode, the genre’s core rhythm alternates “I did X” (earned authority) with “you should do Y” (transferable advice). Self-deprecation buys permission to instruct:

> I was a very mediocre PM at first—I wouldn’t have hired me. But I did learn fast, mostly by watching how the best PMs operated.

> Don’t get overwhelmed by this list. … My advice to you is to skim through the list, pick one habit you want to work on, and focus on that habit for the next two weeks.

## Title × subtitle: hook + promise

Design the pair together. Title = short emotional hook (number, noun, or research question). Subtitle = one-sentence concrete promise: a count, a framework, or a conclusion. Never spend the promise in the title, and never write a vague “some thoughts on X” subtitle — vagueness kills the click and the trust.

> title: “500,000” — subtitle: “Ten lessons learned from building this newsletter”

> title: “How close is AI to replacing product managers?” — subtitle: “Closer than you think”

> title: “Beyond vibe checks: A PM’s complete guide to evals” — subtitle: “How to master the emerging skill that can make or break an AI product”

> title: “Saying no” — subtitle: “A three-step framework for saying no effectively”

The colon pattern “[hook]: [deliverable]” is the default for teaching pieces; “[question]?” plus an answer-teasing subtitle for research pieces; bare number plus “N lessons” is the most reusable milestone formula.

## Opening hook: three steps, three sentences

Step 1: an event or number, with a fact that proves its weight. Step 2: a self-deprecating emotional beat right after the fact — it humanizes the flex. Step 3: pivot to the reader’s benefit (“…is a good time to reflect, so…”). No background wind-up longer than one paragraph.

> This week the newsletter hit 500,000 subscribers, which I’m told makes it among the top five largest newsletters on Substack and one of the largest newsletters broadly. Friggin’ bonkers.
>
> A big round number is a good time to reflect, so I’ve collected some lessons from my journey. I still only sort of know what I’m doing, and it’s unclear where this all goes, but if you’re thinking about embarking on a writing journey, or are just curious about this path, I hope you find this useful.

Announcement variant — enthusiasm plus an immediate invitation to act:

> I’m thrilled to announce that I’ll be hosting a very special one-day conference this fall in San Francisco, on Thursday, October 24. I’ve curated a speaker lineup of product and growth leaders who I deeply respect, and we’re aiming to have 1,000 senior product people in attendance. Want to be one of them? [Apply to attend now]

## Editor’s note: the double-person layer

A piece may open with a fully italicized brand-welcome paragraph — 👋 first, ✨ wrapping “free edition”, identity plus value proposition plus subscription links. Its job: fix the author’s identity, restate the value proposition, and park the promotional material where it can’t interrupt the body. The body voice (guest I, research we, teaching you) never bleeds into this layer.

> *👋 Hey, [Lenny] here! Welcome to this month’s ✨ **free edition**✨ of Lenny’s Newsletter. Each week I tackle reader questions about building product, driving growth, and accelerating your career. If you’re not a subscriber, here’s what you missed this month:*

## Guest intro: who + why credible + where to find them + handoff

Four moves in order: name with link → current role and track record (companies in a row act as a credibility wall) → where to follow them → a fixed handoff phrase (“Enjoy ✨” or “Now, on to the post. . .”). An origin anecdote (“I cold DM’d Patrick and asked him if he’d be up for writing a guest post. He instantly agreed 🙌🙌🙌”) adds warmth and provenance.

> Today’s post is the first of the series and comes from [Amol Avasare], who runs B2B growth at [MasterClass]. Amol was previously an investment banker, chief of staff, and a founder, and last year Amol suffered a serious brain injury. His story of recovery has stuck with me ever since I read the first draft. Enjoy ✨

> Aman Khan runs a popular [course on evals developed with Andrew Ng], is Director of Product at Arize AI (a leading AI company), and has been a product leader at Spotify, Cruise, Zipline, and Apple. … Now, on to the post. . .

Series installments open with full navigation — every part listed and linked, the current part unlinked and marked “← This post” — so any installment works as an entry point:

> Welcome to part five of our ongoing series on how to kickstart and scale a B2B business:
> - **Part 5:** A guide for finding product-market fit *← This post*

## Paragraph progression patterns

**A. Hook three-step.** Fact or number → self-deprecating emotion → pivot to reader benefit. Emotion must follow fact immediately; delay flattens it.

> This newsletter just hit a big milestone: one *million* subscribers. This is both exciting and absurd. No part of me imagined it would reach these heights.

**B. Section machine.** Bold assertion heading → antithetical two-part list (the “matters” side kept brutally short) → mechanism explanation → italic quotation as capstone.

> #### **1. Quality + consistency = all that matters**
>
> Things that don’t matter: your design, your title, your strategy, your growth plan, vision . . . most things.
>
> Things that matter:quality, consistency.
>
> There’s no trick to making something like this grow. Growth comes from publishing something valuable, that people want to share with their friends and colleagues, over and over and over.
>
> *“Strive not to be a success, but rather to be of value”* —Albert Einstein

**C. Empathy → hinge → flip-the-script → checklist.** Validate the reader’s feeling first, cut back to business with a hinge sentence, change seats, land on a numbered list.

> First of all, it’s OK to be the “no” person. It’s literally your job—to create focus and stability for your team and company. …
>
> But that doesn’t answer your question. There’s certainly a skill to saying no, especially when you have to do it often.
>
> Whenever I reflect on a question like this, I try to flip the script and imagine I’m the other person in the situation. As a *manager*, how would I want my report to deal with the barrage of ideas that I throw at them?

**D. Norm vs. exception + worked example.** Describe the default with “Normally…”, introduce the exception with “However, if…”, make it concrete with a named company, close on an interjection (“Magical!”, “Amazing!”).

> Normally, to grow your business, YOU need to go find every new users or customer. For example, if you’re building Google, you need to go tell people about Google… Each additional Google user doesn’t directly drive more Google users.
>
> However, if you’re building a product like DoorDash, Faire, Substack, Dropbox, Eventbrite… a very cool thing can happen: your users grow your business for you. THEY recruit your new users. Magical!

**E. Pain → metaphor → credentials.** Push the reader’s pain to its extreme, offer one unifying metaphor as relief, then buy the right to speak with firsthand scale.

> As a startup founder, you need to do the impossible. You build something that has never been built before, with a team that’s never worked together before… all before you run out of money (and energy). It’s like being lost in the wilderness, in the dark, with only a vague sense of where you’re heading.
>
> Imagine if you had a map.
>
> From TikTok to Reddit to Uber to Airbnb, I’ve had the opportunity to study the growth stories of hundreds of companies.

**F. List first, explain later.** Front-load the entire numbered conclusion list (emoji tails allowed), add a reverse list for contrast, then drill down item by item — the reader gets the whole map in one glance.

> 1. Talking to customers 👂️
> 2. Talking to employees who talk to customers (e.g. sales, customer support, marketing) 🤗
> 3. Observing your customers, through data and user research 🧐
>
> #### Where great ideas rarely come from:
> 1. Large brainstorms — bad source for big new ideas, but has other benefits… 🎯

## Rhythm

- Em dash is the signature mark — insertions and escalations, no surrounding spaces (“work—from the bottom of my heart”); roughly a dozen per piece is normal, not excessive.
- Vary sentence length hard: a long, evidence-laden sentence followed by a two-word punch (“Friggin’ bonkers.”). Repetition as emphasis (“over and over and over”).
- Exclamation marks are allowed and frequent; enthusiasm is part of the genre, not a lapse of taste.
- Parenthetical asides carry jokes and caveats (“(though the numbers aren’t totally accurate)”). Never footnotes — every citation is an inline link, every aside stays in the sentence’s flow.
- Section headings are numbered, bold, debatable assertions (“1. Quality + consistency = all that matters”) so the table of contents reads as a summary. “Introduction” and “Background” are banned headings.
- Data appears as images or screenshots, not Markdown tables; every claim rides a named company, a real number, or a linked source.
- Close with the fixed ritual: thanks or blessing (“Have a fulfilling and productive week 🙏”) → share/subscribe call-to-action → sign-off with name and emoji (“Sincerely, / Lenny 👋”). Promotion goes in a P.S., never mid-body.

## Review checklist: AI and template tells

Reject a draft that shows any of these — in 349 human-written pieces they are near-zero events, so a single occurrence reads as machine output.

- [ ] “delve”, “moreover”, “furthermore”, “In conclusion”, or an “In today’s fast-paced world” opening.
- [ ] Three parallel paragraphs or clauses of identical shape — the triad reflex.
- [ ] Opinions with no named company, no number, no link; real pieces average several links per screen.
- [ ] Footnotes or academic citation format; Markdown tables holding data.
- [ ] An ending that suddenly turns formal, summarizes the piece, or drops the signature ritual.
- [ ] Zero humor, zero parenthetical asides, zero self-deprecation — warmth and self-mockery are the genre’s watermark.
- [ ] Persona drift: editor-layer sentences inside the body, or “we interviewed” claims in an “I” piece.
- [ ] A subtitle that promises nothing concrete — no count, no framework, no conclusion.

## House rule

Use curly quotes and apostrophes in prose (“ ” ‘ ’); straight quotes belong only in code and literal strings.
