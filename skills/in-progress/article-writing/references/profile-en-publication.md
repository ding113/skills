# Publication (English) Style Profile

> **At a glance**
> - A publication is a solemn commitment to the reader: final means final — every sentence must survive being quoted, checked, and reread years later.
> - Register is formal but alive: no internet slang, no over-casual chat, and equally no stiff or showy prose; natural language is not casual language.
> - Verify every fact and every number to its source and clear them item by item; nothing ships until the final checklist is walked with every box ticked.

This genre inherits the Chinese publication editorial system (得到品控手册 + 审校工作规范) in full: the methodology transfers unchanged, and only language mechanics differ.

## 1. What a publication is: a commitment, not a post

- A **blog** is one person's take: personal viewpoint allowed, correctable after the fact.
- **Technical documentation** aims at accuracy now and rolls forward with each version; an error can be patched in the next release.
- A **publication** is fixed at delivery. Readers will quote it, cite it, and build on it. Write every fact, figure, and quotation to the standard of "may be cited verbatim years from now."

Three disciplines follow:

1. Treat every unverified fact as wrong until checked (凡事实必核).
2. Keep terminology, names, and formatting consistent across the whole work — order itself signals professionalism.
3. Avoid vocabulary that dates the text: a publication must still read well in ten years.

## 2. Preface vs introduction (发刊词 vs 导论, KM043)

A long work has two possible openings with entirely different jobs. Decide which one you are writing before drafting a word:

| | Preface ("Lecture 0") | Introduction (Chapter 1) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nature | Sales copy: an invitation and a promise | The first piece of real content: the map |
| Audience | Outsiders who have not yet committed | Readers already inside |
| Temper | Emotional — build the desire to join | Rational — set goals, plan the route |
| Must answer | Why read this now; what you will gain; why this author | Scope (what the work covers and does not, its stance, prerequisites); goal (the overall challenge, the biggest question); structure (which models, what steps) |

The root distinction: **the preface's task is to sell; the introduction's task is to guide** — it explains how the work is modeled (发刊词是销售文案,任务是销售;导论是正式的内容,任务是给课程建模做引导说明).

Mixing them fails both ways: a preface written like an introduction is too hard and kills the desire to join; an introduction written like a preface is too soft and blurs the first chapter's delivery.

Test before drafting: is this passage for people not yet in, or people already in? Promise to the former, map for the latter — never one text for both.

## 3. Title and slogan standards (KM054)

A title or slogan does not chase being loud, novel, or startling; it accurately delivers the work's core value. The craft is not polishing words but digging out the single greatest value the work offers the reader. Work through four dimensions, draft candidates in each, then converge:

1. **Spark curiosity** (outside view): what question does it light up in the reader's mental map? (e.g. "How doctors think" — 医生怎样想事情; "Where does money come from" — 钱从哪里来)
2. **Challenge assumptions** (inside view): what mental model does it overturn — so that even a non-buyer walks away with something? (e.g. "Fewer mistakes means more opportunities" — 犯错少就是机会多; "The examined life is worth living" — 经过审视的人生才值得一过) Pairing something familiar with something unexpected sharpens the challenge.
3. **State the deliverable** (inside view): what is inside, what problem it solves; use contrasts — many vs few, hard vs easy, scattered vs systematic. (e.g. "Read through 52 classics of thought" — 带你读通 52 本思想经典)
4. **Name the outcome** (back to the outside view): who does the reader become afterward? (e.g. "A life that answers to you" — 拥有一个自己说了算的人生; "Stand higher, rethink wealth" — 站在高处,重新理解财富)

Publication-specific constraints: the title must carry the core information, and it must never contain an obscure proper noun that raises the entry bar. Prefer questions to exclamations — a question invites inquiry; an exclamation mark only emits emotion.

Bottom line (KM054): never bait, never mislabel, never promise what the content cannot cash ("take this course and you'll get promoted"). Sincerity is the writer's floor — and the gold line the reader actually feels.

## 4. Register: formal but alive (KM162)

There is no such thing as a "highbrow" work — the service to the reader is between equals (没有什么"高大上"的课程,我们对用户的知识服务是平等的). On that equal footing, remember that natural language is not casual language (自然语言不是口语化). It means three principles:

1. Address one reader, one on one (对象感).
2. Fit the language to how it is consumed — for print, that means silent-reading rhythm: sentences that finish in one breath.
3. Minimize dependence on the reader's prior knowledge.

So: no archaic or ornamental vocabulary deployed to sound learned (violates principle 3); yet respect the author's natural word choices wherever comprehension is unharmed. Hard, elite topics can still be vivid and transparent: state the criteria first, then run familiar, concrete figures through them one by one — done well, the theory explains itself and the register never drops.

## 5. Language rules

- **No internet slang, memes, or fashionable abbreviations.** They rot fast; a publication aims to last. Three years on they read as embarrassment, not warmth.
- **No over-casual chat.** Conversational pull comes from pronouns and well-placed questions, not from slangy diction.
- **Equally, no stiffness.** Do not pile on jargon or ornate phrasing to appear authoritative — plain words that carry the exact meaning beat verbal "glass beads" (掉书袋) every time.
- **Prefer nouns and verbs; ration adjectives.** No purple passages, no manufactured sentiment; restraint reads as strength.
- **Be unambiguous.** A publication states; it does not hint. No subtext, no phrasing that leaves two readings alive.
- **Break long sentences.** Unstack nested logic and mile-long modifiers; if a sentence cannot be read in one breath, split it.
- **End aphorisms with a period, not an exclamation mark.** A period is a sincere invitation to act together; an exclamation mark is a slogan shouted down at the reader.

## 6. Modeling a long work (KM163)

To model is to classify and order facts so the reader gets a simple structure that holds the whole work. Three essentials:

1. **Model because you do not yet understand — not after you do** (不是"因为了解才能建模",而是"正是因为不了解,所以才要建模"). A model expresses your grasp of the reader's concerns, not your mastery of the subject. Build it first from the plainest criteria (chronology, geography, difficulty), then upgrade it as understanding deepens.
2. **Two kinds of models, two owners.** The thought model — the depth of the ideas — belongs to the author or source material; the narrative model — the effectiveness of the telling — belongs to the writer. Competence is finding a narrative model that presents the thought model's depth; mastery is using the narrative model to interact with the thought model and make the ideas themselves better.
3. **Keep stock models on hand**: challenge–solution, space–time, capability–willingness, and above all people–events–artifacts–principles (人事物理). Anchor on the key people; then the pivotal events between them; then the tangible artifacts that give the reader a feel for the field; then the principles — the summit, where concepts and methodology live and the only layer that endures. This model guards against the blind dimension: analyzing a complex subject along a single axis.

Stand with the reader, not with the knowledge. The model exists to help the reader grasp the subject by the most effective route.

## 7. Publication-grade fact-checking

Two iron rules: **every fact verified, every number verified** (凡事实必核,凡数字必核). Operationally:

- **Verify to the source.** Quotations against the original edition; data against the primary source (paper, statistical yearbook, official filing); names of theories and institutions against authoritative references. Never trust a secondhand retelling — you are the one who will be quoted, and the error will be charged to you.
- **Highlight and clear, item by item** (逐条销号). On a read-through, flag every person, date, place, thing, and number; verify one, clear one, until the ledger is empty. An unverified fact is presumed wrong.
- **Four categories that always trip the alarm:**
  - People: names, historical courtesy names and titles, roles, nationality, and who influenced whom.
  - Time: when events happened; when books and articles were published.
  - Places: names, spatial relations, historical changes, and historical vs present-day names.
  - Things: exact wording of quotations, theory names, book/journal/institution names, formulas and units of measure.
- **Numbers, three checks:** precision (18.8% may not be rounded to 20%), arithmetic (recompute every calculation yourself), currency (refresh figures to the publication date).

Consistency review (体例一致) — the twin of correctness; stay hypersensitive to any mismatch:

- One rendering per name across the whole work, no exceptions; if the draft mixes variants, pick one and replace globally.
- One naming scheme per entity — do not alternate between abbreviation and full name at whim.
- Titles of works letter-perfect and identical at every mention; never quoted from memory.
- Numbers consistent across passages; if a quantity changes, say why in the text. "Three takeaways" may not be followed by four.
- One enumeration and heading scheme throughout ("first… second… third", not "first… next… number three").

## 8. Final pre-delivery checklist (from KM132)

Walk every box; all must pass before delivery. Any doubt on any item sends the draft back.

**Required elements (机械动作)**

- [ ] Every chapter opens by locating the reader: what came before, what this chapter covers (the knowledge map).
- [ ] Every chapter closes with a summary: 2–4 points, each at most two sentences.
- [ ] Each chapter hands off to the next, keeping the through-line taut.
- [ ] Structural parts (preface, introduction, chapters, appendices) all present and doing their distinct jobs.

**Cognitive delivery (认知交付)**

- [ ] Every chapter carries the five elements: cognitive starting point (the received view), cognitive end point (the new view), challenge scenario (subject + goal + constraints), perceptible material, and takeaway plus action.
- [ ] No flat forward narration: each chapter opens a challenge the reader can step into, never a concept–theory–application march.

**Base moves (基础动作)**

- [ ] Copyedit pass complete: typos, punctuation, sentence flow, information grouping.
- [ ] Comprehension obstacles cleared: rare words and technical terms translated into language the reader already owns.
- [ ] Fact-check ledger cleared: names, places, titles, numbers, calculations, events, quotations (see section 7).
- [ ] Emphasis disciplined: at most 5 highlighted points per chapter, each at most two sentences; never highlight the opposing view.
- [ ] Chapter scope: one complete cognitive delivery per chapter; if a chapter runs long and cannot be trimmed, re-plan the topic or split it in two.

**Advanced moves (进阶动作)**

- [ ] Thesis thrown down: each chapter states its core question in the plainest possible words.
- [ ] Motivation ignited: an original question exists; one of the four openings applied — hard-to-easy, low-to-high, known-to-unknown, wrong-to-right.
- [ ] Argument built single-layer: at most 4 points per chapter; steady progression, gripping detail, vivid imagery.
- [ ] Junctions serviced: key points flagged in place; questions drive each transition; the reader always knows where they are in the whole.
- [ ] Landing stuck: conclusions drawn explicitly and stated precisely — no hedging, no padding.
- [ ] Logic audited: no fallacies in the reasoning; evidence strong enough (date, scope, freshness); no overlooked alternative cause for the conclusion; no deceptive statistics; no material information omitted.

## 9. Quotation marks

Use curly quotes “ ” in prose, with single curly quotes ‘ ’ nested inside. Straight quotes belong to code samples, not to a publication. When quoting someone else's text, preserve the original's punctuation exactly.
